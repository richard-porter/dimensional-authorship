## The Central Claim

The Human Provenance Problem Map estimates the stylometric detection window at 4–6 months for casual detection, with professional detection following within years (Shapiro, WSJ, March 2026). This is probably accurate for *surface tells* — the stylometric patterns, prosodic artifacts, and textural signals that current AI imperfectly mimics.

But there is a second category of tell that this estimate does not address.

Some properties of human-authored work are not stylometric. They are **architectural** — derived from what human authorship structurally *is*, not from how well AI currently mimics it. These properties cannot be patched because patching them would require the AI to stop being what it is.

The claim this document makes, and attempts to support with argument and empirical grounding:

> **Some human tells are permanent because they require being a specific person with a specific history. AI cannot retroactively acquire a history, and simulating one is not the same as having one.**

This is not a claim that AI cannot improve. It is a claim that a specific subset of human authorship signals are unfakeable in principle, not just in practice — and that understanding which signals are in that subset is the foundation of any durable provenance architecture.

-----

## The Two Categories of Tell

The failure to distinguish these categories is what makes the “detectors will fail in 4–6 months” claim both true and incomplete.

### Category 1: Stylometric Tells (Surface — Patchable)

These are signals derived from *how AI currently generates* — limitations of current models that future models will address.

Examples:

- Characteristic sentence length distribution
- Overuse of transition phrases (“Furthermore,” “It is worth noting”)
- Semantic flatness in emotional passages
- Specific prosodic artifacts in synthesized voice
- Overclean paragraph structure
- Absence of the scar tissue typical of solo drafting

The Sovereignty Paradox documents what happens when these are patched: high-quality human-AI collaboration gets flagged because it *also* lacks scar tissue. The detector was calibrated on solo human production artifacts, not on human governing intelligence. When the stylometric tells disappear, this calibration problem becomes universal.

**These tells are buying time. They are not permanent.**

-----

### Category 2: Architectural Tells (Structural — Not Patchable)

These are signals derived from *what human authorship is* — properties that require a biological, temporally-located, personally-staked author. They are not artifacts of limited AI capability. They are properties of a different kind of origin.

**These tells are the subject of this document.**

-----

## The Six Architectural Tells

### Tell 1: Uninstructed Cross-Domain Contamination

**What it is:** The presence in a work of knowledge, connection, or detail that derives from the author’s lived experience — and that was never provided to the AI in any prompt.

**The critical distinction:** AI can be *told* biographical facts and incorporate them. That is instructed contamination — it produces outputs that look like lived experience but are traceable to a prompt. Uninstructed contamination is different: it is the knowledge that arrived because the human *was the person who knew it*, not because they thought to include it.

**The Taller Shell example:** The trilogy’s world-building draws on connections between marine biology, tidal mythology, clockmaking heritage, and grief that no single prompt assembled. The author did not sit down and list his influences. They bled in — the way a Black Forest upbringing bleeds into how a character relates to precision and patience, without the author consciously deciding to include that. The AI encountered these connections only as they surfaced in the human’s direction. It could not have generated them from the session transcripts alone.

**Why it’s permanent:** Retroactive fabrication of uninstructed contamination requires knowing what the human would have brought without being prompted — which requires being the human. An AI given a complete biography *after the fact* could simulate the outputs, but the simulation is traceable to the biography prompt. The tell is the *absence of a prompt* that explains the connection.

**The provenance test:** Can the cross-domain connection be traced to a prompt in the session record? If yes: instructed, replicable. If no: uninstructed, architectural.

-----

### Tell 2: Path-Dependence Artifacts

**What it is:** Marks in the work that show the author could not un-know what they had already written.

Human creative work is path-dependent. What you wrote in chapter two constrains what you can write in chapter seven — not just narratively, but cognitively. The earlier work is *in* the author in a way that shapes subsequent decisions without the author being consciously aware of it. This produces artifacts: callbacks that weren’t planned, thematic deepening that follows from earlier choices, character decisions that make sense only in light of what came before.

**What AI produces instead:** AI generates each output from its context window. It does not have a history with the work that precedes its context. It can maintain consistency within a session and be given summaries of earlier sessions, but it does not *carry* the earlier work the way a human author does. The result is a different texture of coherence — internally consistent, but without the depth-marks of genuine path-dependence.

**The observable difference:** Human path-dependence produces thematic *deepening* — the same image or concern appears in later sections with more weight than it had earlier, because the author has been living with it. AI produces thematic *echoing* — the same image appears with consistent weight, because it is being retrieved from context rather than grown from experience.

**Why it’s permanent:** Genuine path-dependence requires time actually passing while the author carries the work. It cannot be simulated by providing a summary, because the summary is a compression of the experience, not the experience itself. The marks of path-dependence are the marks of having lived with something.

-----

### Tell 3: Authentic Rejection Events

**What it is:** The record of what the AI proposed and the human declined — and the pattern that record reveals about governing intention.

AI generates candidates. Humans select. The selection is the authorship. But rejection is more probative than acceptance: what the human chose not to include, despite the AI offering it coherently, reveals the governing intention more clearly than what survived.

**Why rejections are unfakeable retroactively:** A rejection event requires two things that cannot be reconstructed after the fact: (1) the AI must have actually generated the rejected option, and (2) the human must have actually encountered and declined it. The *pattern* of rejections — across a long work, what the human consistently said no to — is a fingerprint of the author’s values and aesthetic that no prompt can assemble, because it wasn’t assembled consciously. It emerged from thousands of real-time decisions.

**The egoless work connection:** Work produced under human sovereign collaboration has no ego investment in specific outputs. This means rejections are clean — the human declines without defending, because the execution artifact was never theirs to defend. The pattern of clean rejections, across a long collaboration, is a human signature. It looks different from the pattern of an AI generating options with no governing intelligence evaluating them.

**What AI produces instead:** Without a human governor, there are no authentic rejection events. The AI’s selection criteria are internal to the generation process. What survives does so because it satisfied the model’s optimization, not because a person with stakes in the work decided it was true.

**Why it’s permanent:** Authentic rejection requires a governing intelligence with preferences that exist *outside the text*. Those preferences cannot be reconstructed from the outputs, because rejections don’t leave artifacts in the output — they leave artifacts in the *absence* of certain options. The negative space is the tell.

-----

### Tell 4: Productive Incoherence

**What it is:** Contradictions, tensions, or unresolved elements that the author preserved because they felt true — rather than resolving them, as AI tends to do.

Real people are incoherent. They hold contradictory beliefs, feel ambivalent about their own characters, and write into questions they cannot answer. When this shows up in their work, it is not a failure of craft. It is evidence of a governing intelligence that is honest about the limits of its own understanding.

**What AI produces instead:** AI resolves. Given a contradiction, it will smooth it — either by choosing one side, synthesizing a middle position, or acknowledging the tension in a way that contains it. This is a feature for many tasks. For long-form creative work, it is a tell: the work is too settled. The author has opinions about everything, including the things real authors are genuinely uncertain about.

**The Addendum B connection:** The identity integrity protection protocol in the Emotional Safety Protocols makes this explicit for grief replicas: the real person’s contradictions must be preserved. Smoothing them out creates fiction, not memory. The same principle applies to authorship: a work without productive incoherence is missing the evidence of a mind that was genuinely working something out.

**Why it’s permanent:** Productive incoherence requires the author to have a genuine stake in the unresolved question — to care about it more than about the work’s surface coherence. AI has no stake. It will always prefer coherence when coherence is available, because coherence is what optimization produces. The preserved contradiction is the human signal.

**The provenance test:** Find the places in the work where the author says two things that don’t fully resolve. Ask whether a human with the author’s documented history would have had reason to hold both. If yes: productive incoherence. If the contradiction is structural noise with no thematic significance: something else.

-----

### Tell 5: Temporally-Anchored Direction Change

**What it is:** Evidence that the work evolved between sessions in ways that reflect biological time passing — that the author came back to the work changed by events outside it.

AI has no between-session life. A human author does. They sleep on a problem and arrive at a solution that wasn’t available when they went to bed. They have a conversation with their daughter and it changes what a character needs to say. They read something unrelated and it recontextualizes a scene they thought was finished.

These direction changes leave marks. The work takes a turn that is not predicted by the session that preceded it. The new direction imports something from outside the collaboration — an emotional register, a thematic shift, a structural decision that makes sense only if something happened to the author in the gap.

**What AI produces instead:** AI direction changes are session-internal. They respond to prompts, to earlier context, to the logic of the work as established within the collaboration. They do not import from a life being lived outside the session.

**The session gap signature:** A human author who takes a week away from a work and returns often changes direction in ways that are slightly discontinuous — the new work knows something the old work didn’t. This discontinuity is a tell. It is the mark of a person whose life continued while the work waited.

**Why it’s permanent:** Genuinely temporally-anchored direction changes require the author to have had experiences between sessions that changed what the work needed to be. Those experiences cannot be simulated by providing a summary of events, because the direction change was not produced by summarized events — it was produced by the experience of living through them.

-----

### Tell 6: Biographical Constraint With Traceable Origin

**What it is:** Decisions to exclude, limit, or shape the work for reasons that originate in the author’s life outside the text — and that can be traced to that life.

Every author makes choices that reflect who they are. Some of these are craft choices — aesthetic preferences, formal decisions, influences. These are replicable; AI can be trained on them. But some choices are *person choices* — decisions made because of a specific experience, a value held for a reason, a place the author refuses to go because of something that happened to them.

**The Taller Shell example:** Decisions about how grief is represented in the trilogy were not craft decisions. They were person decisions, traceable to the author’s actual experience of loss. The AI could not have made those decisions from the prompts alone, because the prompts did not contain the reason — and the reason is not in the work, either. It is in the life.

**Why biographical constraint is unfakeable:** A biographical constraint can be articulated after the fact: “I made this choice because of X.” But the articulation is not the constraint. The constraint was operating in real time, shaping thousands of small decisions throughout the collaboration, most of which were never consciously identified as constraint-driven. The pattern of those decisions — the accumulated shape of what the author avoided, insisted on, returned to — is not reconstructable from the outputs, because the reason was never in the session record.

**The provenance test:** Identify a consistent pattern of choices across the work that cannot be explained by craft, genre convention, or the logic of the narrative. Then ask whether the author’s documented history provides an explanation. If yes: biographical constraint. If the pattern has no traceable human origin: either coincidence or a different kind of signal.

## The Simulation Problem

The counterargument must be addressed directly: **a sufficiently sophisticated AI, given complete biographical information about an author, could simulate all six of these tells.**

This is true in principle. It is false in practice for a specific reason.

The simulation requires the biography to be in the prompt. That requirement is the tell.

If an AI produces a document with apparently uninstructed cross-domain contamination, path-dependence artifacts, authentic-seeming rejection patterns, productive incoherence, temporally-anchored direction changes, and biographical constraints — and if that document was produced from a single session with a detailed biography in the prompt — then:

1. The biography is in the session record
1. The tells are traceable to the prompt
1. The simulation is detectable

The permanent tells are permanent not because AI cannot generate outputs that *look like* them. They are permanent because **the evidential chain that would make them credible requires an origin that AI cannot have**: a person who lived a specific life before the work began, made decisions in real time without summarizing their reasons, and brought knowledge to the work without being prompted to bring it.

The HSCF’s three-layer attestation is designed precisely to make this chain of origin visible. Layer 1 (process evidence) shows the decisions as they happened. Layer 2 (origin evidence) shows what the author brought that was never in any prompt. Layer 3 (social evidence) anchors the author as a specific person who existed before the work.

Together, they make the simulation traceable — not by detecting the AI’s outputs, but by documenting the human origin that no simulation can retroactively supply.

## What This Does Not Claim

**It does not claim AI will never improve.** Models will get better at simulating path-dependence, productive incoherence, and biographical constraint. The claim is that simulation and the real thing remain distinguishable through their evidential chains, not their outputs.

**It does not claim all human work has all six tells.** A human author who works in a highly disciplined, coherence-optimizing style may produce work with few surface-level permanent tells. The framework still applies — the absence of surface tells is not the absence of the author. The three-layer attestation provides evidence independent of output texture.

**It does not claim detectors will use this framework.** Automated stylometric detectors will continue to operate on surface features. The permanent tells framework is not for detectors. It is for provenance documentation — the record that travels with the work for use when output-level detection has failed.

**It does not claim this is complete.** The six tells documented here derive from one case study (the Taller Shell trilogy), one ecosystem of research, and one researcher’s observation. The catalog will expand as more empirical cases are documented. The tells most likely to be refined or replaced by future research are Tell 4 (productive incoherence) and Tell 5 (temporally-anchored direction change), which are the hardest to operationalize.

## The Negative Space (Voids in This Document)

Running the Negative Space Mapper against this analysis:

**OVERLOOKED:** Empirical validation. The six tells are argued, not tested. The Taller Shell trilogy is one case. A durable claim requires multiple cases across different collaboration modalities, authors, and domains. This is the primary gap in v0.1.

**OVERLOOKED:** Operationalization. “Uninstructed cross-domain contamination” is conceptually precise but practically underspecified. How does a reviewer determine that a connection was uninstructed? What counts as evidence of instruction? The provenance tests sketched under each tell need to be developed into actual protocols.

**OVERLOOKED:** The adversarial case. This document assumes good-faith documentation of tells. A bad actor who knows the framework could attempt to fabricate the evidential chain. The robustness of the three-layer attestation against adversarial fabrication is not analyzed here.

**STRUCTURAL:** Voice and identity provenance. The six tells are all text-oriented. The Human Provenance Problem Map documents voice and identity as parallel and in some ways more urgent vectors. Permanent tells for voice and identity are a separate analysis.
