## Gap 1: Consent Scope

### The Problem

HSCF v0.1 certifies that a human was governing. It does not yet address whether the human knew what they were governing.

A human-led work can contain AI-generated elements the human didn’t fully register — passages where conductor fatigue set in, sections where the AI’s execution register bled into the human’s voice without the human noticing, moments where the human approved rather than selected. The governing intention was present. The awareness of specific AI contributions may not have been.

This is not a failure of authorship. It is a gap in the consent architecture. The question is not “was a human deciding?” but “was the human aware of what they were deciding about?”

Addendum B (Emotional Safety Protocols) names this precisely in the posthumous ethics context: consent requires awareness of scope. The same principle applies to authorship. A human who approved AI output without registering it as AI output has not fully consented to the collaboration in the way the HSCF’s certification claims.

### The Architecture

Consent scope operates at three levels. The HSCF’s three-layer attestation maps onto each:

**Level 1 — Awareness of AI participation (Layer 1: Process Evidence)**

The minimum viable consent: the human knew AI was involved and was making decisions about its output. This is established by the process log — the existence of rejection events, redirect events, and constraint declarations is evidence that the human was aware of and engaging with AI-generated material as AI-generated material.

Binary test:

> Does the process record show evidence of the human *treating AI output as AI output* — evaluating it, selecting from it, declining it — rather than absorbing it without deliberate engagement?

PASS: Rejection and selection events exist and are documented.  
FAIL: No process record; or process record shows only acceptance events with no evidence of deliberate evaluation.

**Level 2 — Awareness of scope (Layer 1 + Layer 2: Process + Origin Evidence)**

The human was aware not just that AI was involved, but of *what the AI contributed* versus what the human brought. This is the distinction between instructed and uninstructed cross-domain contamination (Permanent Tells, Tell 1): the human can identify what they provided and what the AI generated.

This level is not required for certification — many legitimate human-led collaborations involve the human not tracking every contribution granularly. But it is required for the strongest certification claim, and its absence should be disclosed.

Binary test:

> Can the author identify, at section level, what they brought to the work versus what the AI generated or expanded?

PASS: Author can distinguish their contributions from AI contributions at a meaningful level of granularity.  
PARTIAL: Author has general awareness but cannot distinguish at section level.  
FAIL: Author cannot distinguish their contributions from AI contributions at any level.

**Level 3 — Awareness of register (Layer 2: Origin Evidence)**

The human was aware when the AI’s execution register was influencing the work’s voice — and made a deliberate choice about whether to retain it, modify it, or remove it. This is the Register Contamination gap (identified from the Tell Engine data, addressed in the permanent tells addendum): the AI exoskeleton problem, where polished AI prose bleeds into and over human-governed sections.

Binary test:

> When the work contains AI-influenced register (explanatory over-clarity, symmetrical rhetorical structure, concept labeling), was that a deliberate choice rather than unconsidered absorption?

PASS: Author can identify AI-register sections and confirm they were intentional.  
PARTIAL: Author is aware of AI register in some sections but not others.  
FAIL: Author was unaware of AI register influence; cannot distinguish it from their own voice.

### The Consent Scope Score

Consent scope adds a fourth dimension to the HSCF’s three-layer attestation — not a separate layer, but a question that runs across all three:

> *Was the human aware of what they were governing, at the level required to claim what they are claiming?*

A full certification claim (human-led with AI assistance, all five properties present) requires Level 1 consent at minimum. Level 2 is recommended. Level 3 is required only when the work contains significant AI-register sections that are being presented as the author’s own voice.

## Gap 2: The Adversarial Use Case

### The Problem

HSCF v0.1 is written for the author who initiates the claim: “I want to certify that this work was human-led.” The burden of proof runs forward — the author assembles evidence, the framework provides the structure.

The adversarial use case runs in the opposite direction: someone has challenged the work’s human origin, and the author must now defend it. The burden of proof has been imposed rather than assumed. The author may not have been maintaining a process log. The social evidence may be thin. The challenge may be based on a miscalibrated detector (that’s Gap 3) or on genuine suspicion.

These are different problems from proactive certification, and they require a different instrument.

### The Adversarial Response Protocol

When a work is challenged on human origin grounds, the response has three phases — in order, without skipping:

**Phase 1: Characterize the challenge**

Before responding to a challenge, establish what is actually being claimed. Challenges come in three forms:

|Challenge type       |What it claims                                           |What evidence addresses it                                                                   |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|**Output challenge** |The work has AI-like stylometric properties              |Sovereignty Paradox — the heuristic is miscalibrated; see Gap 3                              |
|**Process challenge**|There is no evidence a human was governing               |Layer 1 (process evidence) — produce the decision record                                     |
|**Origin challenge** |The specific human claimed as author did not produce this|Layer 2 + Layer 3 (origin + social evidence) — produce voice fingerprint and external anchors|

Responding to an output challenge with process evidence wastes time and concedes framing. Characterize first.

**Phase 2: Match evidence to challenge type**

Output challenge response:

> The Sovereignty Paradox documents that effective human-AI collaboration eliminates the stylometric artifacts that detectors were calibrated on. The absence of scar tissue is evidence of successful collaboration, not synthetic authorship. The governing question is not “does this have AI tells?” but “who was governing?” The following evidence addresses the governing question directly: [Layer 2 origin evidence — cross-domain contamination, ROSETTA baseline, biographical constraint].

Process challenge response:

> The process record for this work is available for review. It documents [rejection events / redirect events / constraint declarations / session gaps] across [timeframe]. The pattern of these decisions is consistent with a human governing intelligence with a specific set of values and aesthetic commitments, not with AI generation without human governance. [Layer 1 evidence].

Origin challenge response:

> The following evidence locates [author name] as the specific sovereign person who governed this work: [copyright registration date / contemporaneous correspondence / workshop records / ROSETTA baseline predating the collaboration]. The cross-domain knowledge present in the work derives from [specific biographical sources] that were never provided in any session prompt. [Layer 2 + Layer 3 evidence].

**Phase 3: Name the gap honestly**

If the evidence for the challenged dimension is thin — if the process record is incomplete, if the social evidence is sparse, if the ROSETTA baseline predates the collaboration by only weeks — say so. A partial defense that acknowledges its own limits is more credible than a complete defense that overreaches.

The proactive gap disclosure principle applies in the adversarial case: name the void before the challenger names it for you.

### What the Adversarial Case Reveals About Proactive Certification

The adversarial protocol makes explicit what proactive certification should be building toward. A process log maintained during collaboration is not bureaucratic overhead — it is the evidence that makes Phase 2 possible. A ROSETTA baseline established before the collaboration begins is not vanity — it is the origin evidence that survives an origin challenge.

The adversarial use case is the reason to maintain the three-layer attestation prospectively, not just invoke it retrospectively.

## Gap 3: False Negative Protection

### The Problem

This gap is different from Gap 2. An adversarial challenge involves a human making a claim about authorship. A false negative involves an automated or trained system making a classification error — flagging human-led work as synthetic because the detector’s calibration is wrong.

The Sovereignty Paradox names the mechanism precisely: detectors were calibrated on solo human production, which had characteristic execution-capacity artifacts. Effective collaboration eliminates those artifacts. The detector then penalizes success. This is a measurement problem, not an authorship problem.

But the consequences of a false negative are real: a publisher rejects the work, an institution flags it for misconduct review, a submission system auto-rejects. The author needs a response that addresses the measurement problem without conceding the authorship question.

### Why False Negatives Are Different From Adversarial Challenges

An adversarial challenge is made by a person who has a theory about the work’s origin. It can be contested on the merits — evidence can be produced, the claim can be examined, the challenger can update.

A false negative from an automated detector cannot be contested on the merits of the detector’s reasoning, because the detector has no reasoning — it has a score. The author is arguing against a number, not a claim. This requires a different response strategy.

### The False Negative Response Framework

**Step 1: Identify what the detector actually measures**

Most stylometric detectors measure one or more of:

- Sentence length distribution
- Vocabulary range and frequency
- Transition phrase patterns
- Semantic coherence across paragraphs
- Absence of execution artifacts (scar tissue)

None of these measure governing intelligence. All of them measure surface properties that effective human-AI collaboration has been trained — by the Sovereignty Paradox — to eliminate or reduce.

Before responding, establish which properties triggered the score. If the detector’s methodology is not published, note that absence as a gap in the detector’s own credibility.

**Step 2: Apply the Sovereignty Paradox directly**

The Sovereignty Paradox is the documented, named, empirically-grounded explanation for why this detector result does not measure what it claims to measure in the context of human-AI collaboration. It is citable. It has a case study. It has a governing question.

> “The detector flagged [specific properties]. These properties are documented artifacts of effective human-AI collaboration in the Sovereignty Paradox framework (AI Collaboration Field Guide, March 2026), which established empirically that high-quality collaboration eliminates the execution artifacts that stylometric detectors were calibrated on. The absence of scar tissue is evidence of successful collaboration. The governing question — *if the AI vanished, would the work still have had a soul, just less range?* — is answered by the following evidence: [Layer 2 origin evidence].”

**Step 3: Produce the structural evidence the detector cannot measure**

A detector operating on output properties cannot measure:

- Uninstructed cross-domain contamination (Tell 1)
- Authentic rejection events (Tell 3)
- Productive incoherence (Tell 4)
- Biographical constraint with traceable origin (Tell 6)

These are the permanent tells — the structural properties that derive from being a specific person with a specific history. Produce evidence for each that is present in the work. The detector’s score addresses none of them.

**Step 4: Name the detector’s calibration gap**

Close with the proactive gap disclosure principle applied to the detector:

> “This detector was calibrated on solo human production. It has not been validated against human-led AI collaboration as a distinct authorship modality. Its false positive rate for this modality is unknown. The score it produced is not evidence of synthetic authorship; it is evidence that the detector’s training distribution does not include the collaboration modality this work represents.”

This is not an attack on the detector. It is an accurate characterization of its measurement limits — the same discipline the HSCF applies to its own gaps.

### The Deeper Issue

False negatives from miscalibrated detectors are not a temporary problem that will resolve as detectors improve. The Sovereignty Paradox suggests the opposite: as AI improves and collaboration quality increases, the false negative rate for human-led collaboration will *increase* — because the output will become less distinguishable from AI generation at the surface level, while remaining structurally distinct at the permanent tells level.

The long-term solution is not better detectors. It is detectors that measure governing intelligence rather than output properties — which requires the three-layer attestation infrastructure to exist, so that detectors have something to measure beyond the artifact.

The HSCF is not just a defense against false negatives. It is the architecture that would allow future detectors to be calibrated correctly.

## Updated Negative Space (Post Gap Closure)

Running the Negative Space Mapper against the HSCF with these additions:

**CLOSED:** Consent scope (Gap 1), adversarial use case (Gap 2), false negative protection (Gap 3).

**STILL OPEN:**

**OVERLOOKED:** Real-time voice provenance. The HSCF covers documents. The Human Provenance Problem Map’s Vector 2 (voice) remains unaddressed. This is a separate instrument.

**OVERLOOKED:** Trust model mapping — who needs what verified, under what conditions, with what consequences for failure. This is the next document. The adversarial response protocol (Gap 2) begins to sketch it, but the full mapping across novelist, journalist, clinician, court, patient, and reader is not here.

**OVERLOOKED:** Detector validation standards. Gap 3 characterizes why current detectors fail for collaboration modalities. It does not specify what a correctly calibrated detector would need to measure or how it would be validated. That is architecture beyond this framework’s current scope.

**STRUCTURAL:** Empirical validation of the three-layer attestation under adversarial conditions. The adversarial use case (Gap 2) is described as a protocol, not tested as one. Whether the evidence layers are actually sufficient to survive a well-resourced challenge is unknown.
